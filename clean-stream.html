01 / Context

The ABA clinical trinity — and why data integrity is a patient safety issue

Action Behavior Centers delivers one-on-one Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) therapy for children with Autism Spectrum Disorder. Therapeutic progress depends on a continuous, unbroken loop of real-time behavioral data flowing across three interdependent roles.

RBT

Registered Behavior Technician

Logs behavioral data during active therapy sessions — often while physically managing challenging child behavior simultaneously.

BCBA

Board Certified Behavior Analyst

Uses RBT session data to build and advance individualized treatment plans. Unusable data means no plan progression — directly stalling a child's development.

CD

Clinical Director

Approves treatment plans and owns HIPAA compliance and billing accuracy across the center.

The stakes: When session data is missing or corrupted, BCBAs cannot advance treatment plans. Children's therapeutic progress stalls. Clinicians burn out. And the organization faces regulatory and billing exposure. This was not a UX inconvenience — it was a clinical operations crisis.

02 / The Strategic Pivot

Reframing the problem from "who" to "what"

Before any research began, leadership had already formed a hypothesis. Understanding — and then dismantling — that hypothesis was the most consequential research decision I made on this project.

Initial Assumption

"RBTs aren't following clinical plans because they're too complex."

→
Researcher Discovery

"RBTs are abandoning data entry because the UI is incompatible with the physical reality of therapy."

This pivot emerged during Week 3 shadowing sessions. While observing RBTs in active therapy, I noticed that data entry essentially stopped the moment a child became dysregulated. Technicians had to choose between managing the child safely and logging data — and every time, they correctly chose the child.

The research insight that changed everything: "Dirty Data" is a survival mechanism, not a compliance problem. Solving it required redesigning the data collection experience — not retraining the clinicians. Cognitive Load Theory gave me the language to make that argument credibly to both clinical and engineering leadership.

03 / Research Design

Why these methods — not just what they were

Method selection was driven by a single constraint: the most critical behaviors happen in the room, during active sessions, when RBTs are least able to articulate their experience. This ruled out surveys and unmoderated testing as primary methods and demanded observational, naturalistic approaches.

Qualitative — The "Why"

Contextual Inquiry

Shadowed RBTs across full 4-hour therapy sessions.

↳ Chosen because self-report would systematically miss the "hands-busy" moments where data quality collapsed.

Diary Studies

RBTs logged post-session frustration over 3 weeks.

↳ Chosen to capture longitudinal fatigue and identify which session types correlated with churn spikes.

1-on-1 Depth Interviews

BCBAs interviewed individually to surface data artifacts blocking plan advancement.

↳ Individual sessions prevented BCBAs from deferring to each other's judgment in group settings.

Quantitative — The "What"

Usability Benchmarking (SUS)

Standardized scores for internal data-collection tools.

↳ SUS scores translate across clinical and engineering audiences without requiring domain expertise.

Task Success Rate & Error Logging

Measured mis-tap frequency during simulated high-energy events.

↳ Distinguished UI design flaws from environmental constraints — both require different interventions.

Time-on-Task Measurement

Quantified per-event logging latency across all interaction states.

↳ Established a concrete 12-second baseline that made the problem legible to engineering and product leadership.

04 / Participants & Research Operations

55 participants across three clinical roles

35 RBTs 10 remote · 25 in-clinic
10 BCBAs Primary synthesis users
10 CDs Plan approvers

RBTs were required to have completed more than 10 sessions per week using the current clinical workflow, with direct involvement in both batch-logging and real-time logging. Including remote RBTs was deliberate — remote delivery introduced distinct constraints that in-clinic shadowing alone could not surface.

ResOps note: Standard vendor recruitment was not viable in this HIPAA-compliant clinical environment. Participants were recruited offline via Google Sheets synchronized with clinical calendars. All research protocols and data handling were conducted in compliance with HIPAA requirements. No patient data was recorded or retained.

05 / Field Findings

The "Dirty Data" lifecycle — a four-phase degradation pattern

Across shadowing sessions, a consistent hour-by-hour pattern emerged in how data quality degraded during a standard therapy block. This was structurally reproducible across the cohort — which is what made it actionable as a design problem rather than a training gap.

Phase Hour 1: Setup Hour 2: Intensity Hour 3: Fatigue Hour 4: Catch-up
UI Event 4 taps required per log Tablet physically abandoned for child safety Mental notes substituted for logging Mass retrospective entry ("data dump")
Data Quality High accuracy Non-existent Ambiguous Dirty / back-filled
RBT Emotion Optimistic Stressed Drained Frustrated

Core finding: Data quality collapsed precisely during high-intensity behavioral episodes — the most clinically important moments to capture. The UI required 4 taps per log when the RBT had zero free hands. Dirty data was not negligence. It was a rational coping mechanism under impossible cognitive and physical load.

BCBA interviews confirmed the downstream consequence: back-filled data produced timestamp distributions indistinguishable from accurate data. BCBAs couldn't tell which records were real-time and which were reconstructed — making the entire dataset suspect for plan-advancement decisions.

06 / AI-Accelerated Synthesis

Building a custom analysis pipeline with Cursor AI and RStudio

55 participants across three roles generated a substantial body of transcripts, diary entries, and observational notes. Manual coding would have taken 3–4 weeks and introduced inter-rater reliability concerns. Instead, I built a custom pipeline.

1

Transcript preparation

All 55 transcripts and diary entries were de-identified and structured for programmatic processing. Remote RBT sessions were transcribed from recorded video calls; in-clinic sessions from field notes.

2

Custom R code generation with Cursor AI

I used Cursor AI to write custom R scripts for sentiment detection and keyword frequency analysis tailored to the clinical lexicon. Rather than applying off-the-shelf NLP trained on general text, the scripts were designed around domain-specific frustration indicators — phrases that a general model would not flag as high-signal.

3

Frustration keyword quantification

Scripts scanned transcripts for validated frustration-signal phrases, mapping frequency against session phase to confirm the hour-by-hour degradation pattern. This triangulation between observational and verbal data strengthened confidence in the core finding.

4

Lexicon misalignment mapping

Term-frequency analysis across RBT vs. BCBA transcripts surfaced systematic vocabulary divergence — RBTs used field-naturalistic language while the UI used clinical taxonomy. This became a discrete, actionable recommendation for the product team.

5

Human validation at every stage

All AI-flagged patterns were manually reviewed against source transcripts before being treated as findings. The AI pipeline accelerated pattern detection; human judgment determined what those patterns meant and whether they warranted a recommendation.

07 / Co-Design Validation

From research insights to product-ready recommendations — with the people who'd use them

Co-design workshops (Wk 9–12) brought RBTs, BCBAs, and CDs into the same room to validate AI synthesis findings and stress-test proposed solutions before any engineering investment. Four distinct needs emerged, each traceable to specific field observations.

Language gap — clinical vs. field vocabulary

RBT Need · BCBA Need

The UI used BCBA clinical taxonomy throughout. RBTs used different everyday language for the same behavioral events — creating systematic miscoding as RBTs selected "close enough" terms.

Output: Participants mapped RBT-natural language onto BCBA clinical terms and identified 12 high-frequency terms driving consistent miscoding.

High-behavior moments — the "hands-full" capture problem

Primary RBT Need

During high-behavior episodes, the tablet was set aside. All behavioral data for these moments — often the most clinically significant — was lost or reconstructed from memory.

Output: A one-tap behavior flag that timestamps the moment instantly, with an ABC prompt before session note submission capturing context while memory is fresh.

Data loss prevention — real-time save access

Secondary RBT Need

RBTs were losing in-progress data to accidental navigation, app timeouts, or device failures. A save function existed but required navigating away from the data entry context.

Output: A persistent "Save Progress" button positioned directly adjacent to the data collection fields — zero navigation, zero cognitive overhead.

Missing data visibility — the accountability layer

BCBA Need · CD Need

BCBAs and CDs had no way to identify missing session data or trace which RBT conducted a specific session. Missing data was often discovered weeks after the fact.

Output: A Data Tracker dashboard surfacing missing session records with RBT-to-client traceability — enabling same-day follow-up while memory is intact.

08 / Recommendations

Four prioritized interventions — each grounded in user evidence

1
Priority 1 · Real-time capture

One-Tap Behavior Flag + ABC Nudge

A single large-target button timestamps a behavioral event the moment it occurs, freeing the RBT to manage the child immediately. Before session note submission, a structured ABC prompt ensures clinical context is captured. Target: under 1 second to log; ABC completion under 2 minutes.

↳ Contextual inquiry (hours 2–3) + BCBA interviews: "ABC data is almost always missing for high-behavior sessions"

2
Priority 2 · Data integrity

Persistent "Save Progress" Button

A persistent, zero-navigation save action anchored adjacent to data entry fields. Single tap, no confirmation dialog. Eliminates session data loss from navigation errors, app timeouts, or device failures during extended sessions.

↳ Diary studies (data loss events) + RBT co-design: "I need it to be as easy as blinking"

3
Priority 3 · Vocabulary alignment

Unified Clinical Lexicon

Replace BCBA-centric clinical taxonomy throughout the RBT data entry experience with field-naturalistic language mapping to the same underlying clinical codes. 12 high-frequency terms identified as primary miscoding drivers. BCBAs see clinical terms in their analytical views; RBTs see field language in their entry screens.

↳ AI lexicon misalignment analysis + co-design vocabulary mapping exercise

4
Priority 4 · Accountability infrastructure

BCBA / CD Data Tracker Dashboard

A real-time dashboard for BCBAs and CDs showing missing session records with client-to-RBT traceability. Enables same-day follow-up on data gaps while the RBT's session memory is still accurate. Reduces the lag between a missing data event and recovery from weeks to hours.

↳ BCBA depth interviews + CD stakeholder sessions during co-design workshops

10 / Reflections

What I'd do differently — and what I learned

What worked well

Applying Cognitive Load Theory as a reframing device was the single most effective strategic move. It gave engineering and clinical leadership a shared mental model for why "bad data" was a design problem, not a people problem. The AI synthesis pipeline compressed a month of manual coding into a week without sacrificing rigor, because I maintained human validation at every stage.

What I'd do differently

I would include remote RBTs in shadowing from Week 1, not Week 4–5. Their distinct constraint set — device positioning, physical space management — surfaced the one-tap solution. I'd also involve CDs in co-design from the beginning rather than primarily at the validation stage. Their accountability lens shaped the Data Tracker requirement in ways that could have influenced earlier decisions.

The HIPAA constraint as a design teacher

The Voice NLP solution initially explored in synthesis was compelling — and ultimately not viable within compliance and engineering constraints of the immediate roadmap. Working through that constraint with Legal and Dev led to the ABC Nudge solution, which achieved better clinical outcomes within existing infrastructure. Constraints forced a better answer.

What this changed about how I run research

Users cannot tell you what they do when their hands are full — they tell you what they wish they did. Shadowing across the full session lifecycle, including the unglamorous end-of-session "data dump" hour, was the only way to see the real problem. Full-lifecycle observation is now non-negotiable for any high-stakes workflow research I run.
