Case Study · RAD Lab, SF State · 2022–2024

The Enthusiasm Gap

Why verbal interest didn't become digital sign-ups — and what the data revealed about a portal that was destroying enthusiasm it didn't create.

01 / Context

The platform had traffic. It didn't have participants.

The RAD Lab runs federally-funded cognitive psychology research at SF State. Its participant pool — undergraduate students earning extra credit — is the engine keeping grant funding alive. Enrollment quotas feed grant reporting cycles. Falling short didn't just mean fewer participants: it put the next funding phase at risk.

The portal had footfall. It didn't have enrollments. And the initial hypothesis from the lab pointed squarely at the students.

Initial Assumption

"Students weren't interested enough to follow through after the recruitment talk."

→
Researcher Discovery

"The website was actively destroying interest that already existed — through information overload, buried CTAs, and a paradox of choice."

Business stakes: Enrollment quotas feed grant reporting cycles. Falling short didn't just mean fewer participants — it put the next funding phase at risk. Framing this as a conversion funnel problem from the start created immediate stakeholder buy-in with grant administration.

Cafeteria field recruitment captured students mid-abandonment — in the environment where the decision not to sign up was actually happening. This produced richer data than a lab setting because participants were still actively experiencing the friction rather than recalling it.

Q

"Attended a research talk in the last 6 months?"

Ensured participants had experienced the full funnel — ruling out those never exposed to the conversion opportunity.

Q

"Interest in paid academic studies: 4–5 / 5?"

Isolated the gap between genuine intent and follow-through — filtered out disinterest as an explanation for non-conversion.

Q

"Mix of Yes / No on 'visited the RAD website'"

Compared those who reached the portal vs. those who never arrived — testing whether the problem was pre-portal or portal-specific.

04 / Field Findings

3 critical failures — none of them were about student motivation

Behavioral data, interviews, and heatmaps all pointed to the same conclusion: the website was actively destroying enthusiasm that students arrived with. The problem wasn't disinterest — it was design.

01

The Wall of Science

The landing page opened with dense academic abstracts. Students felt they had to read everything before they were "allowed" to sign up — a gatekeeper that didn't exist in reality but was communicated loudly by the UI.

↳ "I felt like I had to earn the right to sign up"

02

The Hidden Gateway

The sign-up CTA was below the fold — 3+ scrolls required. Heatmaps showed rage clicks on non-interactive text elements as students searched for the entry point.

↳ Heatmap: zero clicks above the fold on the CTA region

03

The Paradox of Choice

Multiple repeated links pointing to the same destination made students second-guess which was "right." Hesitation at the point of action killed momentum that had survived everything else.

↳ Think-aloud: 6 of 8 participants verbalized confusion on which link to click

Stakeholder challenge: Researchers framed academic abstracts as "transparency." Research reframed it with heatmap data: the "transparency" content was causing the exact drop-off it meant to prevent. The solution preserved transparency through an expandable section — nobody had to lose anything.

Diary studies revealed the conversion window post-recruitment talk was shorter than anyone assumed. Students who didn't sign up within 24–48 hours almost never returned. Memory decay — not sustained disinterest — was eliminating late conversions.

05 / Recommendations

4 interventions — each traceable to evidence

1
Priority 1 · CTA Visibility

Action-Oriented Hero — CTA above the fold

Move the sign-up CTA above the fold as the primary action. Academic abstracts moved to an expandable section — transparency preserved, friction eliminated. Students who want detail can access it; students who want to sign up no longer have to earn the right to do so.

↳ Heatmap (zero clicks above fold) + interviews: "I felt like I had to earn the right to sign up"

2
Priority 2 · Cognitive Load

Quick-Match Study Filter

Filter studies by available time slot directly on the landing page. Eliminates the cognitive overhead of browsing studies that don't fit a student's schedule — which was causing mid-funnel abandonment even after students had decided to participate.

↳ Diary studies: students returned looking for "a study that fits my Tuesday"

3
Priority 3 · Form Abandonment

Progress Bar in Sign-Up Flow

Visible progress indicator during multi-step registration. Reduces form fatigue and abandonment at the final stage — which was the last point of drop-off even for students who had navigated the landing page successfully.

↳ Usability testing: "I didn't know how much more there was. I just quit."

4
Priority 4 · Decision Clarity

Consolidate Duplicate CTAs

Single, clearly labeled sign-up entry point throughout the site. Eliminates the paradox of choice at the final decision moment — 6 of 8 think-aloud participants verbalized confusion about which link was correct.

↳ Think-aloud: 6 of 8 participants verbalized confusion about "the right link"

06 / Impact

Measured outcomes, with baselines

62% Increase in sign-up conversion

Measured against pre-implementation baseline. Driven primarily by moving the CTA above the fold and consolidating duplicate sign-up entry points.

92% Faster to find the sign-up link

Time-to-CTA reduced from 140 seconds to 12 seconds. Direct result of removing the Wall of Science as the first encounter on the page.

40% Decrease in bounce rate

Landing page bounce rate dropped 40% post-launch. Action-oriented hero and Quick-Match filter gave students an immediate reason to stay.

$ Grant funding secured on schedule

Participant quotas met — the downstream business outcome that protected the lab's next funding phase and validated the research investment.

07 / Reflections

What worked, what I'd change

What worked

Framing as a conversion funnel problem (not student motivation) created immediate stakeholder buy-in. Cafeteria field recruitment captured students mid-abandonment — richer than a lab setting. Mixed-method triangulation prevented over-indexing on vivid interview quotes. Diary studies revealed the memory decay arc: the conversion window post-talk was shorter than anyone had assumed.

What I'd change

I'd involve the data scientist in synthesis earlier — RStudio work ran in parallel rather than in dialogue with qualitative themes. I'd also address the stakeholder alignment tension (academic transparency vs. UX simplicity) with earlier workshops. Graduate students were underrepresented — their funnel experience may have differed meaningfully from undergrads.

Biggest lesson

The data a team uses to build a product often reflects what was easy to collect — not what users actually do. The RAD Lab had clickthrough data but no behavioral observation. Research gave the lab an evidence base it didn't know it was missing, and turned a student motivation problem into a design problem that was entirely solvable.

On stakeholder pushback

Researchers resisted removing the academic abstracts, framing it as a transparency commitment. The turning point was showing heatmap data: the "transparency" content was causing the exact drop-off it meant to prevent. Evidence-first communication worked where persuasion alone would have failed. The expandable section solution meant nobody had to lose anything.
